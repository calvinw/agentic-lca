Great — let's just talk through it. I'll use a super simple fictional example throughout: a disposable coffee stirrer — plastic vs. wooden.

Phase 1: Goal & Scope
Before any numbers, you decide what you're studying and why.

Goal: Compare the environmental impact of a plastic stirrer vs. a wooden stirrer.

Functional unit: One stirrer used once to stir one cup of coffee. This is the key move in LCA — you pick a unit that makes both options fairly comparable. Both stirrers do the same job, so one stirrer = the basis of comparison.

System boundary: We'll go cradle-to-grave — meaning we follow the stirrer from raw material extraction all the way to it being thrown away. We'll exclude things like the factory building itself, or the consumer driving to the coffee shop — those are too indirect.

Impact categories: We'll track just two:

Global Warming Potential (GWP) in kg CO₂-equivalent
Water use in liters
Simple enough? Now Phase 2 is where you actually collect data.

Phase 2: Inventory Analysis
This is just a list of everything that goes in and comes out for each stirrer, across each life stage. Think of it as a recipe + waste log.

Life Stage	Plastic Stirrer	Wooden Stirrer
Raw material	1.5 g polypropylene (from oil)	2.0 g wood (from tree)
Manufacturing energy	0.010 MJ	0.006 MJ
Water used	0.05 L	1.2 L (wood pulping)
Transport energy	0.001 MJ	0.001 MJ
CO₂ emitted (mfg)	0.8 g	0.3 g
End-of-life	Landfill	Landfill (or compost)
These are made-up but plausible numbers. In real LCA you'd look these up in a database like ecoinvent. Notice wood requires way more water — pulping and processing wood is wet.

Phase 3: Impact Assessment
Now you convert those raw inventory numbers into comparable environmental scores using characterization factors. These are published by scientists — they answer "how bad is 1 g of this thing, in terms of CO₂-equivalent?"

GWP calculation:

Flow	Plastic	Wood	Factor	Plastic GWP	Wood GWP
CO₂ from mfg (g)	0.8	0.3	0.001 kg CO₂-eq/g	0.0008	0.0003
Thermal energy (MJ)	0.010	0.006	0.056 kg CO₂-eq/MJ	0.00056	0.00034
Electrical energy (MJ)	0.001	0.001	0.100 kg CO₂-eq/MJ	0.0001	0.0001
TOTAL GWP				~0.0015	~0.0007
So the wooden stirrer has roughly half the GWP of plastic.

Water use:

Water is simpler — 1 liter consumed = 1 liter impact (CF = 1).

Plastic: 0.05 L
Wood: 1.2 L
Wood uses 24× more water.

Phase 4: Interpretation
Here's where you make sense of the numbers:

On climate: Wood wins — lower GWP because wood stores carbon and manufacturing is less energy-intensive than making plastic from petroleum.
On water: Plastic wins — by a huge margin. Wood processing is very water-intensive.
No clear winner — which is the honest, typical LCA conclusion. The "better" choice depends on what you care about most and where you are (water-scarce regions would weight water heavily).
The pedagogical punchline: LCA forces you to be specific. "Paper/wood is natural so it's better" is too vague. Better for what? Better where? LCA makes those tradeoffs visible and quantifiable.

Great question. The three parts of a Life Cycle Inventory (LCI) are:

Inputs — things that go into the process: raw materials, energy, water
Outputs — things that come out: the product itself, co-products
Emissions — releases to the environment: CO₂ to air, wastewater to water, solid waste to land
The third one is sometimes called elementary flows or releases to nature — they're distinguished from outputs because they go to the environment rather than to another process or to the customer.

So in the stirrer example:

Input: 1.5 g polypropylene, 0.010 MJ energy, 0.05 L water
Output: one stirrer (the product)
Emissions: 0.8 g CO₂ to air, solid waste to landfill
The distinction between outputs and emissions matters because outputs can sometimes be used again (co-products, recycling) whereas emissions are just gone into the environment. That's also why end-of-life is tricky — a landfilled stirrer is an output that becomes an emission source (methane from decomposing wood, microplastics from plastic).

In the spreadsheet I built, the "Emissions to Air" section at the bottom of the Inventory sheet is exactly this third category.

You're right — the more standard term you've probably seen is releases or more commonly just waste — but the most precise term used in LCA standards (ISO 14040/14044) is:

"Elementary flows"

which covers both inputs from nature (like water withdrawn from a river) and outputs to nature (like CO₂ emitted to air). So the three-part breakdown in ISO language is:

Inputs from nature — elementary flows going in (raw materials, water, land)
Inputs/outputs between processes — intermediate flows (energy, materials between unit processes)
Outputs to nature — elementary flows going out (emissions to air, water, soil)

Sure — let's go through each one on the left side of the graph.

Land use (0.05 m²·yr)

This means we're counting the physical area of forest that had to be occupied for one year to grow the wood that eventually became your cup. The unit m²·yr means "one square meter for one year." It's a small fraction because a cup only uses a few grams of wood — but across billions of cups it adds up. Land use matters because occupied land can't be a natural habitat, so it represents a loss of biodiversity and ecosystem services.

Freshwater withdrawn — pulping (13.1 L)

This is water drawn from a river, lake, or aquifer and used in the pulping process. Pulping is how you turn wood chips into paper pulp — you essentially cook wood in hot water and chemicals to break the fibers apart. It is extremely water-intensive. Most of this water gets contaminated in the process (which is why BOD shows up as an output), and some evaporates. "Withdrawn" is the key word — it means taken out of a natural water body, not returned to it clean.

Freshwater withdrawn — cooling (1.2 L)

Separate from pulping, manufacturing machinery generates heat and needs cooling water to keep it from overheating. This water is also drawn from nature. It's much smaller than the pulping figure but still counted as an input from nature because it's extracted from a natural source.

Crude oil (2.1 g)

This covers two things rolled into one. First, the polyethylene coating on the inside of a paper cup — that thin plastic lining that makes it waterproof — is made from petroleum. Second, some of the energy used in manufacturing comes from burning fossil fuels. Both ultimately trace back to crude oil being extracted from the ground. We're counting the raw resource extracted from nature, before it gets refined into plastic or fuel.

The common thread across all four: these are things that come directly out of nature — no human process created them. Land, water, and oil all existed before the cup did. That's what makes them elementary flows rather than intermediate flows. In contrast, "electricity from the grid" is an intermediate flow — it was produced by another human process (power generation) before arriving at the factory.

Sure — the four outputs on the right side of the graph, each one released from the system into nature.

CO₂ to air (4.0 g — manufacturing)

This is carbon dioxide emitted directly from burning fossil fuels during the manufacturing process — running the machinery, generating steam for pulping, heating the factory. It goes straight into the atmosphere. This is the most familiar elementary flow and the one that drives the Global Warming Potential score in Phase 3. The 4.0 g is just the direct manufacturing emission — it doesn't yet include the upstream CO₂ from generating the electricity used (that gets captured through the electricity process flow).

NOₓ to air (0.02 g — transport)

NOₓ is a family of nitrogen oxide compounds — mainly NO and NO₂ — produced when diesel fuel burns at high temperature in a truck engine. The nitrogen in the air reacts with oxygen under the heat of combustion. It's a relatively small quantity per cup but matters because NOₓ contributes to smog, acid rain, and respiratory problems. In a full LCA you'd also track it as a contributor to a separate impact category called "photochemical ozone formation" — not just global warming.

CH₄ to air (0.8 g CO₂-eq — landfill)

This is methane, produced when paper decomposes in a landfill. Landfills are largely anaerobic — no oxygen — so organic material like paper breaks down through bacterial fermentation rather than normal decomposition, and the byproduct is methane rather than CO₂. Methane is about 28 times more potent as a greenhouse gas than CO₂ over 100 years, which is why we express it in CO₂-equivalent units. This is one of paper's hidden costs — it looks biodegradable, but biodegrading in a landfill produces a more damaging gas than if it had just been incinerated.

BOD to water (0.3 g — pulping effluent)

BOD stands for Biological Oxygen Demand. When the wastewater from pulping gets discharged into a river or water body, it carries dissolved organic matter — wood sugars, lignin fragments, residual chemicals. Bacteria in the water consume that organic matter, and in doing so they consume oxygen. BOD is a measure of how much oxygen will be depleted from the water by that process. High BOD in a waterway kills fish and aquatic life because it starves the water of dissolved oxygen. So BOD is essentially a proxy for "how much does this discharge suffocate the river."

Solid waste to land (11.6 g — cup body)

This is simply the physical mass of the cup ending up in a landfill. The cup weighs about 11.6 g and at end of life it goes to land. It's counted as an output to nature because landfill space is a finite natural resource being consumed. It's also what generates the CH₄ above — so these two outputs are connected: the solid waste going to land is what decomposes into the methane that goes to air.

Notice that two of the outputs — CH₄ and solid waste — both come from the same end-of-life event. That's a common pattern in LCA: one process can generate multiple elementary flows simultaneously. In openLCA you'd see both listed as outputs of the landfill unit process.

The five processes in the centre column — these are the unit processes that make up the product system, connected by intermediate product flows.

Forestry & pulp production

This is the starting point. Trees are grown, harvested, and processed into wood pulp — the raw fibrous material that paper is made from. The intermediate flow leaving this process and entering the next one is 10.1 g of wood pulp. What makes this a process rather than an input is that it was produced by human activity — someone operated a forestry operation and a pulping mill. The elementary flows attached to this process are the land use and crude oil inputs we just discussed, plus some CO₂ emissions from the logging machinery.

Cup manufacturing

This is where the pulp gets turned into an actual cup — paper is formed, cut, shaped, and coated with a thin polyethylene lining to make it waterproof. This is the most input-heavy process in the system — it draws in the pulping water, cooling water, and electricity, and it's where most of the direct CO₂ and BOD emissions happen. The intermediate flow leaving this process is one finished cup.

Distribution

The finished cup gets packed and trucked from the factory to a warehouse or retailer. This process consumes diesel fuel — modeled here as 0.005 MJ of road transport energy per cup. That's a tiny number because the cost is shared across millions of cups in a single truckload. The elementary flow leaving this process is the NOₓ emission from the diesel combustion. The product flowing out is still the cup — unchanged physically, just moved.

Use

One hot drink is served. From an LCA perspective this is almost a pass-through — the cup itself doesn't change chemically during use, no significant emissions happen, and no natural resources are consumed by the cup alone. The main reason it's included as an explicit unit process is that it anchors the functional unit. This is the moment the cup actually performs its function — serving one beverage — which is what the whole system exists to do. The product flowing out is the used cup.

Landfill disposal

The used cup enters the waste system and ends up in a landfill. This process has no intermediate product flowing out — it's the terminal node of the system. What it does produce are two elementary outputs to nature: the methane from anaerobic decomposition, and the solid mass occupying landfill space. In a more complete LCA you might branch this into alternatives — composting, incineration, recycling — each of which would be a separate process with different elementary flows attached.

The key insight across all five: each process receives an intermediate flow from the one above, does something to it, and passes an intermediate flow to the one below. The elementary flows branch off sideways at each step — inputs arriving from nature on the left, outputs leaving to nature on the right. That branching structure is exactly what the product graph shows visually.

Together the three categories give you the complete picture of what the system touches: what it takes from nature, what it does internally, and what it gives back to nature. That's the full inventory.

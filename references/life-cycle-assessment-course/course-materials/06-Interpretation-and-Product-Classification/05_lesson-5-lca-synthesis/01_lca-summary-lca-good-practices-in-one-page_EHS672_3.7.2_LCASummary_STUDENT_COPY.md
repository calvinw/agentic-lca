### GOAL & SYSTEMDEFINITION
- 1c. System boundaries
- System boundaries include all the necessary processes to assure the desired function.
- Rule 1: System boundaries must cover the same functional reality in all scenarios.
- Rule 2: A cut-off should be defined, where processes can only be excluded if they contribute less than the cut-off percentage (e.g. 1%).
- 4. Interpretation
- Interpretation should be carried out at all levels, looking at:
- the contribution of each life cycle stage and process, to prioritize improvements that generate the largest impact at the lowest cost;
- the contribution of each substance;
- each phase of the life cycle assessment;
- uncertainty to identify if scenarios are significantly different.
- 3. Impact assessment
- The impact assessment quantifies the impact of each emission and extraction to enable comparing scenarios, and the contributions of different life cycle stages, substances and impact categories.
- Impact assessment methods must at least incorporate the fate (transport, dilution and degradation) and effects (e.g intrinsic toxicity, soil and species sensitivity) of emissions and extractions.
- More sophisticated methods accounting for spatial differentiation should be used when it substantially reduces variability.
- Several impact assessment methods should be used in parallel to test results robustness.
- Use financial allocation based on market value as a measure of the incentive for production.
- Use arbitrary assumption without causality only if necessary.
- 2c. Input-Output
- The input-output approach deconstructs the functional unit into industrial spending to create the inventory.
- 2. Inventory
- 2a. Definition
- The inventory is the quantified elementary flows of matter, energy and substances that cross the system boundary, including pollutant emissions and extracted resources.
- 2b. Allocation and co-products
- Avoid allocation through process separation (if it is possible to dissociate the different product systems) or through system expansion (if it is possible to extend the system boundaries to include a proven substituted product and avoided impacts).
- Use physical allocation based on mass or energy content only if there is a direct causality, or use marginal allocation when it is possible to marginally vary the proportion between co-product and product.
- General Principle
- It is recommended to carry out the LCA in two steps:
- A first screening step covers the whole LCA and assesses the order of magnitude of emissions, resource use and impacts.
- A detailed analysis improves the assessment for the most important impacts.
- Goal and system definition
- 1a. System function:
- The LCA assesses the environmental impacts associated with a product function. Products or systems can only be compared on the basis of a similar function.
- 1b. Functional unit andreference flows:
- The functional unit is the common unit in all scenarios, quantifying the function of the system.
- Emissions and extractions are calculated per functional unit. It is not a ratio.
- The reference flows differ for each scenario, representing the amounts of goods or services purchased to generate one functional unit.
- Lifetime, number of uses, use phase efficiency and other parameters linking reference flows to functional unit can all be key parameters for environmental optimization.
- Rule 3: Processes that are identical in the different scenarios can only be excluded if the intermediary flows of these processes are strictly equal (i.e. total output of each system must also be identical).

- Summary
- The LCA system approach is a strength, able to screen and identify hotspots across product life cycle, looking at multiple impacts
- Data availability for both inventory and impact assessment coverage has substantially increased and facilitate application
- Uncertainty remains high and need to be well-acknowledge and accounted for when informing decisions
- A one product at a time might not bring us to sustainable consumption

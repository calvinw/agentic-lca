Some complementary information on when to include service duration in the functional unit:

As a general rule, when what you need to buy per functional unit changes as the service duration is extended, you need to make the service duration explicit in the functional unit. Also when the usable lifetime of the product you buy: for the shelter function offered by a house, the amount of energy needed and of material also since we will need renovation over 50 years and the house last more than 1 year changes whether I use it for 1 year or for 50 years and whether the house is well built or not. In that case, you can arbitrarily choose a most convenient service duration of either 1 year or 50 years, and then determine for the reference flows the FRACTION of the house needed for 1 year service, which might be 1/25 of a house if the lifetime of the house is 25 years.

On the other hand – Some functional unit already implicitly account for the duration of the service. For example for energy a functional unit of 1 J (= 1W∙s) is already a power of 1 W during one second and implicitly account for the service duration (note that it is a Watt DURING a second expressed mathematically by a multiplication, it is NOT a Watt PER s. having a "/" in a functional unit is unlikely since this is a ratio that is not an additive unit. We also do not need to give the service duration when it is irrelevant or might not matter, i.e. if what you need to buy per functional unit does not change if the time extends: for example, for comparing a wedding ring in gold or silver, both last more than the duration of an even long marriage years of 60 years (whereas you could argue that you can reuse your grandparents ring and then it might matter if e.g. gold is more resistant than silver).

And remember the functional unit does not represent what you buy (these are the reference flow), but the service provided by the product you buy! So first think – what is the function the service offered!
